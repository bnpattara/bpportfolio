Executive summary

The fashion recommendation problem is not an algorithm problem. It’s a trust problem. Consumers don’t distrust AI because it’s wrong; they distrust it because it’s visibly designed to sell rather than serve. Meanwhile, every major retailer employs expert human stylists whose reach ends at the shop floor door.

Stylect closes that gap. A shopper submits a one-minute brief; an algorithm surfaces three matched stylists; the shopper picks one; within 24 hours they have a three-item shortlist with a human-written reason for each pick. The platform earns on outcome (affiliate revenue at checkout, direct commission to the stylist, a retailer partnership fee) which aligns every incentive in the chain toward the purchase that actually fits.

01The problem

Discovery is broken.
Because e-commerce is optimized for engagement, not outcomes.

Discovery is tuned for engagement and retailer margin, not for the moment a shopper needs a decisive judgment. People feel that misalignment: and still need a human to say this one.

Expert stylists hold real trust on the floor, but that authority doesn’t transfer online at scale. The feed gets infinite reach; the person who actually knows the rack doesn’t.

02Insight / Idea
Insight
People trust people, not platforms
People have never trusted platforms. They trust people who happen to work at them. The entire history of retail loyalty runs through individual relationships: the associate who remembers your name, who pulls something off the rack because she thought of you. E-commerce scaled the store and deleted the person. It kept the inventory and removed the one thing that made it navigable: a human being with a stake in your outcome.
Idea
AI filters the brief · A person owns the pick
Stylect uses AI to filter a universe of inventory against a specific brief, then hands the recommendation to a real, in-store stylist who provides the judgment that converts a browser into a buyer. Speed of discovery is traded for confidence of outcome. Human authorship is not a feature of the platform: it is the platform.

04The business model

Three parties.
All three only win on a completed transaction.

Stylect is a three-sided marketplace: the shopper, the stylist, and the brand manager. The platform earns affiliate revenue at checkout. The stylist earns a direct commission. The retailer receives guaranteed, highly qualified foot traffic. Nobody earns anything on clicks or browsing behavior.

What each side brings · what they earn

Shopper

Brings discovery and intent. Earns a human-named shortlist: not another engagement feed.

Stylist

Brings taste and trust. Earns commission when the recommendation converts, online or in store.

Brand

Brings inventory and traffic. Earns qualified visits and closed-loop attribution, not anonymous clicks.

Stylect · platform layer

Attribution ties the sale to the stylist. Curation + reservation + commission only fire on a completed transaction: never on scroll depth.

05The design system

Every design decision was made to make trust legible.

Most fashion interfaces treat design as expression, brand flex, aesthetic signal, a reason to linger and keep you scrolling through an environment. Stylect is built through a molecular design system that aligns all tokens to perform a specific function in the trust sequence.

The system is built to WCAG AA contrast standards, as a design constraint so visual noise never substitutes for clarity. Elevated without being exclusive, legible for every user.

06What shifted

Not better recommendations. Accountable ones.

For the first time, a stylist’s judgment has a direct line to attribution. Two hours to a shortlist. A QR code that closes the loop at purchase. A commission that proves the conversation drove the sale. The stylist’s labor (previously invisible the moment a shopper left the store) becomes measurable and compensable at scale.

That’s not a product feature. That’s a structural correction to how retail expertise gets valued. Stylect doesn’t just help shoppers find the right item in under 24 hours. It makes the person who found it for them worth paying.
