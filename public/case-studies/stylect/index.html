Executive Summary

The fashion recommendation problem is not an algorithm problem. It's a trust problem. Consumers don't distrust AI because it's wrong; they distrust it because it's visibly designed to sell rather than serve. Meanwhile, every major retailer employs expert human stylists whose reach ends at the shop floor door.

Stylect closes that gap. Shoppers submit a one-minute occasion brief from home. An algorithm surfaces the three best-matched, available in-store stylists. The shopper selects one. Within 24 hours, the stylist has responded with a three-item shortlist — each with a personal note explaining the reasoning. The shopper reserves their favorite, receives a QR code, and walks into the store already knowing what they're there for. Every step is tracked. Every transaction is attributed.

The business model is affiliate revenue at checkout, commissions to the stylist, and a retailer partnership fee. The platform earns on outcome, not engagement — which aligns every incentive in the chain toward the purchase that actually fits.

01The Problem

Two failures. One gap. A 24-hour window to close it.

The consumer side of the failure is visible: choice paralysis. The average fashion app serves thousands of SKUs through an algorithm calibrated for engagement, not confidence. The shopper scrolls, abandons, returns, scrolls again. The 73% cart abandonment rate in fashion e-commerce is not a checkout problem. It's a decision-confidence problem. The algorithm optimizes for click; the shopper needs someone to say this one.

The retailer side is structural: underutilized expert labor. Every major retailer employs in-store associates with genuine product knowledge, styling intuition, and real relationships with the inventory. Their reach ends at the door. A stylist at Nordstrom Short Pump can help forty people per shift. Stylect turns that same expertise into a digital channel — extending the stylist's reach to shoppers on their couch, building their brief before they ever set foot in the store.

The insight that grounds the entire product: recommendation failure is not a data problem. Every major fashion platform has more behavioral data than any stylist will ever collect. The failure is structural — the recommendation system is optimized for the retailer's margin, not the consumer's confidence. Once you name the real problem, the solution space changes completely. You're not building a better algorithm. You're building a trust infrastructure.

02The Insight
“Algorithm proposes. Human closes. The platform earns on outcome — not engagement. That changes every incentive in the chain.”

The structural insight is this: the two sides of this problem have mirror-image failures. The consumer has data abundance and trust scarcity. The stylist has trust abundance and reach scarcity. Stylect is not a technology product. It's an infrastructure product that connects two entities who already have what the other needs, and gets out of the way.

The business model follows directly from this thesis. Affiliate revenue at checkout means Stylect only earns when a confident purchase is made — not when a user clicks, browses, or adds to cart. This is an unusual incentive design in fashion tech, where every other platform maximizes engagement. Outcome-based revenue aligns Stylect's interest with the shopper's goal: one right item, quickly, with confidence.

Insight 01 · The Trust Gap
Human
Closing the last step of a fashion recommendation requires a human voice. Not because the algorithm is wrong, but because trust is interpersonal. The "why-it-works" note is the product.
Insight 02 · The Attribution Gap
QR
The QR code isn't a checkout feature. It's the measurement infrastructure that closes the online-to-offline loop. Every in-store reservation is tracked. Every stylist commission is attributed.
Insight 03 · The Incentive Model
Outcome
Revenue only at purchase. Affiliate fee covers CAC. Stylist earns commission. Retailer pays platform fee for in-store conversion. Every participant wins only when the shopper wins.

The second-order insight is about the retailer's Brand Manager, who becomes the third user type in this ecosystem. Before Stylect, the Brand Manager has no data on how online discovery translates to in-store visits. After Stylect, every QR scan is attributed, every stylist's performance is ranked, every product's keep/return rate is visible in real time. The platform produces measurement infrastructure that didn't exist before — which is why the Brand Manager Dashboard (F10) is a CORE feature, not supporting.

05Design System

A molecular design system built for three user contexts.

The design system was built alongside the PRD, not after it. Every token, component, and pattern was derived from a specific user need documented in the feature requirements — not from aesthetic preference. The system has two modes: light mode (the default for all app screens) and dark mode (reserved exclusively for onboarding screens 1.1–1.3, then washed out in a single 800ms transition as the user enters the app).

Four typefaces carry four distinct roles in the product. Cormorant Garamond 300 is the wordmark only — upright, architectural, no decoration. Cormorant Garamond 400–500 carries display copy and editorial weight — stylist names, occasion titles, shortlist card names. Didact Gothic handles all body copy and UI text. Space Mono carries metadata: prices, dates, labels, status indicators. No typeface crosses into another's territory.

Token System
2
Color modes: Light (app default) and Dark (onboarding only). One-way transition — light never returns to dark mid-session.
Component Depth
3
Hierarchy levels: Atoms (buttons, chips, inputs) → Molecules (stylist card, shortlist card, chat bubble) → Organisms (full phone screens).
Radius System
8
Radius values from r-0 to r-pill (999px). Cards use r-lg (16px). Buttons use r-pill. The wordmark and rule elements always use r-0 — editorial never rounds.
Glass Surfaces
3
Glass Light (72% white), Glass Warm (80% ecru), Glass Dark (68% void black). Used over photography and gradients — never on flat solid backgrounds.
PM Lens · Design System as Infrastructure

The design system is not a style guide. It's a decision log in visual form. Every token represents a tradeoff: pill radius on buttons signals accessibility without sacrificing editorial quality. The mandatory "why-it-works" italic quote style in shortlist cards enforces the product's core mechanic at the component level — a PM decision rendered in CSS. When the system and the PRD agree, the product is coherent.

07Decision Log

What we built, what we deferred, and why.

Product decisions are only credible when they're documented with the tradeoff accepted, not just the choice made. This log covers every significant decision in the PRD, design system, and prototype — including the ones that were cut.

What We Didn’t Build & Why
Not Built · Out of Scope
The Browse Grid
Every fashion app has a browse grid. The browse grid is the problem. Users don't need more ways to see 12,000 SKUs. They need one way to get to the right one. Removing the browse grid from home is the first design decision that signals what Stylect actually is.
Not Built · Out of Scope
Scarcity & Urgency Mechanics
"Only 1 left in your size" — the manipulation pattern that defines fast fashion UX. Stylect's trust premise depends on the absence of urgency mechanics. The SLA timer on the stylist workspace is a service commitment, not a pressure tactic.
Not Built · Out of Scope
Cart Badge During Live Session
The cart badge is hidden during any active chat session. Commerce is the epilogue, not the context. Showing a cart badge during a conversation about what to buy conflates the shopping experience with the styling experience — they must be sequential, not simultaneous.
Not Built · Future
Real-Time Inventory API (F8)
Full inventory bridge across 200+ brands requires retailer API infrastructure that is expensive to build before the platform proves unit economics. MVP uses simplified inventory search within partner stores. F8 ships when there are enough partner transactions to fund the integration cost.

“The product doesn’t end at the screen. It ends at the fitting room mirror — and the QR code is the thread that connects every pixel to that moment.”

What this project demonstrates is the discipline of constraint. The PRD makes 14 hard decisions, documents 7 success metrics with measurement methodology, and draws a clear line between MVP and roadmap. The design system translates those decisions into a visual language with 20 documented sections, a two-mode system, glass surface components, and a type hierarchy that enforces the product's core mechanic at the component level. The prototype builds seven interactive screens across three user types without a single speculative feature.

The argument this case study makes for PM practice: product clarity is not a function of how many features you ship. It's a function of how clearly you can explain why each feature exists, what it trades against, and what it would cost to defer it. Every decision in this document is one or the other — never just "it felt right."
