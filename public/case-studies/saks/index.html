Executive summary

Saks built its dominance on one thing: the authority to curate. Designers wanted placement. Consumers trusted the edit. That position collapsed the moment brands could go direct to consumer and no longer needed a middleman to reach the market. Without the curation advantage, Saks was left carrying the full cost structure of the old model: $4.7B in debt, 180‑day buy cycles, and sprawling fixed‑cost flagships against declining foot traffic and a designer community that had quietly stopped sending their best work.

Vault rebuilds the authority from the balance sheet up. It eliminates owned inventory, replaces fixed leases with flex nodes, and converts the physical store from a cost center into a data collection engine. Members pay for elite early access. Designers pay for purchase intelligence that arrives 12 weeks ahead of any wholesale signal in the market. The result is a platform that makes Vault the most valuable place in luxury retail to be for the consumer who wants curation, and for the designer who needs to know what she is buying next.

01Problem & insight
The problem
Saks lost its reason to exist before it lost its money.
When brands went direct, the aggregation model collapsed. Without curation authority, Saks was a $4.7B debt load attached to sprawling fixed‑cost flagships, a six‑month buy cycle that couldn’t match cultural velocity, and hundreds of millions in unpaid vendor invoices that ensured the best designers stopped showing up.
The insight
Luxury was never about the store.
It was always about who decided what you could have. Saks built its authority as the curator whose selection conferred desirability. That authority didn’t disappear when foot traffic dropped. It transferred to whoever controlled access. The consumer who matters isn’t looking for more options. She is paying for fewer, better ones, chosen for her before she has to ask.
02The solution

The Curation Engine: Accuracy at Scale

Vault replaces the legacy cost structure with three interlocking revenue streams.

Stores
Flex nodes, zero owned inventory
26 buying cycles per year vs. industry ~2
The physical footprint shrinks to 3,500‑square‑foot flex nodes on month‑to‑month leases with zero owned inventory. Designers retain ownership until the moment of sale. The node resets every 14 days, driven by four cultural signals: social velocity, editorial coverage, resale premiums, and prior rotation performance. Twenty‑six buying cycles a year against an industry standard of two.
Membership
Access architecture, not a loyalty program
Member $500 · Reserve $900 · Black $4,500
Three tiers priced against a specific consumer relationship with curation: Member at $500, Reserve at $900, Black at $4,500. Membership covers fixed node costs before a single item sells. Retail becomes upside.
Platform
Designers pay for intelligence
12 weeks ahead of wholesale signals
Designers pay to participate in exchange for real‑time sell‑through data and member aggregates that arrive 12 weeks ahead of any wholesale signal in the market. Designer subscriptions generate a third revenue stream that scales with the node network. The retailer–designer relationship inverts. Vault becomes a data platform that happens to sell clothes.

Trust is rebuilt through architecture. Smart contract settlements execute payment three days after each drop. An 18‑month backlog becomes structurally impossible.

04Designer partnership

Designer platform · dashboard & data architecture

Every rotation generates data. Every data point improves the next curation decision. Designers subscribe for sell‑through velocity, anonymized member aggregates, rotation scoring, and settlement status — intelligence that wholesale never shares.

What this screen does

Designers can see exactly how fast each piece is selling. That information arrives before they need to commit to their next run (something a traditional wholesale buyer would never share).

01 / 05
What this screen does

Anonymous style profile aggregates across Reserve and Black tier members. Informs silhouette, color, and fabrication decisions before the sample is cut. Designers act on this 12 weeks earlier than wholesale buyers can.

02 / 05
What this screen does

Each designer can see how their pieces are performing compared to everyone else on the floor that rotation. It creates a natural incentive to send the best work. The designers who drive members back to the store get more floor space next cycle.

03 / 05
What this screen does

Payment within 14 days of sale, verified through smart-contract settlement. No net‑90 risk. Settlement at this speed is faster than any comparable luxury retailer and the mechanism that rebuilds vendor trust in one rotation cycle rather than one fiscal year.

04 / 05
What this screen does

Bi-weekly cultural signal digest: social velocity, editorial coverage, resale premiums. Proprietary intelligence included in the platform subscription. Designers act on demand signals weeks before wholesale buyers receive trend reports.

06Verdict

Own the data. Not the building. Not the inventory.

Vault proves that the most defensible position in luxury retail is owning the data. Not the building, not the inventory. The purchase signal.

That position is only possible because of two relationships rebuilt from scratch. Designers trust Vault because payment is guaranteed by architecture, and because the sell‑through data makes their next collection more accurate than any wholesale signal in the market. Consumers trust Vault because the decision has already been made for them.
