Executive summary

The legacy 42,000 sq ft store model is structurally obsolete: it carries too much owned inventory and depends on mass foot traffic. Vault replaces it with a membership model built around small 3,500 sq ft stores that swap their entire inventory every 14 days, charging designers a subscription to show their product and members a membership fee to shop. The shift is from a store that passively stocks designer labels to a membership that actively manages and activates a relationship between designers and the customers who matter most to them.

Houses already own direct-to-consumer (DTC) and first-party data. Vault earns its place by tracking what members actually do (which pieces they rotate, request, and hold) and making that behavior visible to designers in a way that a traditional wholesale relationship never can. Paying designers within 14 days of each sale and giving them a direct platform to their consumer is what makes designers actually want to be here, while giving awareness to new markets and alignment with other luxury brands.

Insight
Inventory & footprint
Luxury retail taught customers that the store was the destination. Then it forgot to give them a reason to show up. Foot traffic fell, inventory sat for seven months on average, and the customer relationship never got past the transaction.
Idea
Vault
Replace the store with a membership. Small stores, 14‑day inventory rotations, no owned stock. The building is no longer the product; access is. Members pay to shop early. Designers pay to reach them. Floor sales are the upside, not the foundation.
Impact

Eliminating owned inventory, long leases, and vendor debt saves an estimated $964M per year. The $123.4M transformation pays itself back in under two months, because the entire cost structure was the problem, not the brand.

Saks didn’t go bankrupt because the brand lost its taste. It went bankrupt because it kept betting on a customer behavior (showing up to a 42,000 sq ft store to browse) that had already quietly stopped. The Chapter 11 filing was the financial proof of a cultural shift that had been happening for years.
01 Balance sheet & filing

When customers stopped showing up at the rate the model needed, every other problem got worse at once. The post-merger debt load of $4.7B had been manageable as long as the stores stayed full, but with ~$600M/year in projected synergies that never arrived, owned inventory that wouldn’t turn, and lease obligations that didn’t flex, each slow month hit the same balance sheet. The filing was the end of a chain reaction, not the beginning of one.

02 Buying cadence & inventory

The deeper problem was timing. By the time a buyer committed to inventory, six months would pass before it reached the floor, and culture had already moved on. What was trending on social media, in editorial, and on resale markets when the buy was placed looked nothing like what customers wanted when the goods arrived. That mismatch is why stock sat for ~210 days on average, markdowns followed, and margin never recovered. Vault’s 14‑day rotation exists specifically to close that gap.

03 Vendor terms & trade trust

Designers noticed before anyone else did. When payment terms stretched from 90 days to effectively never ($700M+ in unpaid invoices, an 18-month backlog), the best designers quietly redirected their strongest pieces to retailers who paid on time. Saks kept getting the second tier. When you can’t pay your suppliers, the product suffers, and when the product suffers, customers stop coming. Vault’s consignment model and 14‑day settlement isn’t a financial perk: it’s the thing that makes designers want to show up with their best work.

04 Footprint, traffic & demand

The store map was drawn for a customer who no longer exists at the volume the model needed. Store rents like ~$9.2M per 10,000 sq ft only make sense when enough people walk through the door, and with traffic down ~14% year over year, the math stopped working while the lease stayed fixed. At the same time, roughly 65% of high-net-worth Gen Z luxury spending happens in cities where Saks has no presence at all. The footprint was built for where wealthy customers used to live, not where they are now.

05 Digital distribution

The last attempt at a fix made the brand problem worse. The $475M Amazon partnership chased distribution at the exact moment luxury customers were moving toward exclusivity. Mass marketplace availability told the customer that anyone could access Saks, which is the opposite of what luxury sells. Vault operates on owned infrastructure only, with no mass marketplace presence, because the insight here is that access controlled by the brand is worth more than reach controlled by a platform. Full Legacy vs. Vault comparison is in the Appendix.

02Tiered membership model

Nodes, tiers, and the human engine

Vault replaces legacy store square footage with ~3,500 sq ft nodes: month-to-month lease, modular build, designer-owned stock on the floor. Access runs through three tiers (Member, Reserve, and Black), each with earlier access, more stylist time, and at-home delivery for the members who spend the most.

“Most retailers sell products to people. Vault sells access, and uses what members do with that access to get better at finding them what they actually want, providing valuable market data to subscribed designers.”

Identity scales to each market; the node stays small by design.

03Cultural velocity

Buying cadence matches the feed, not the season

Traditional department stores commit to their inventory six months before it hits the floor. Vault refreshes every 14 days, using what's trending on social media, what editors are covering, and what's selling for a premium on resale to decide what comes in next. That's 26 buying cycles a year versus the industry's average of about two and a half. If an item doesn't sell in one market, it gets moved to another market at the end of the 14 days.

06Designer partnership

A platform for designers · data in exchange for consignment

Designers keep ownership until sale. Vault does not take inventory risk. In exchange, designers receive real-time data on what’s selling and why: anonymized member behavior, how their pieces rank against the rest of the rotation, and bi‑weekly cultural trend signals.

What this screen does

Designers can see exactly how fast each piece is selling. That information arrives before they need to commit to their next run (something a traditional wholesale buyer would never share).

01 / 05
What this screen does

Anonymous style profile aggregates across Reserve and Black tier members. Informs silhouette, color, and fabrication decisions before the sample is cut. Designers act on this 12 weeks earlier than wholesale buyers can.

02 / 05
What this screen does

Each designer can see how their pieces are performing compared to everyone else on the floor that rotation. It creates a natural incentive to send the best work. The designers who drive members back to the store get more floor space next cycle.

03 / 05
What this screen does

Payment within 14 days of sale, verified through smart-contract settlement. No net‑90 risk. Settlement at this speed is faster than any comparable luxury retailer and the mechanism that rebuilds vendor trust in one rotation cycle rather than one fiscal year.

04 / 05
What this screen does

Bi-weekly cultural signal digest: social velocity, editorial coverage, resale premiums. Proprietary intelligence included in the platform subscription. Designers act on demand signals weeks before wholesale buyers receive trend reports.

Relationship over traffic

The brands that survive the next decade of luxury will not be the ones with the most square footage. They will be the ones that structurally prioritize the depth of the relationship over the breadth of traffic. Vault is the argument that knowing your customer deeply and running a leaner business are not in conflict; they're the same move.

Cadence as product

Not owning inventory isn't just a financial decision: it's what makes it possible to change the entire store every two weeks without losing money when a trend shifts. Designers earn market data either way and continue to have access to node rotation when items don't immediately sell. 14‑day rotation replaces the six‑month buy with a loop the org can learn from every cycle. Both run on the same 14‑day cycle; the rotation is the product cadence and the financial unit at once.
