Executive Summary

On Running makes some of the most technically precise footwear in the market. The product is not the gap. The gap is that running, as most people experience it, is a solitary act, and solitary acts don't build communities. On Apex is the platform infrastructure that closes that gap: a community PWA that converts the lone runner narrative from a default into a choice, and builds the kind of brand belonging that no product alone can create.

02Background

On entered the market as a Swiss-engineered performance brand with a distinctive product philosophy and grew rapidly on the strength of its technology and aesthetic credibility. Its consumer base skews toward committed runners and design-conscious urban professionals, people who care about how they perform and how they look doing it. The product has built genuine loyalty. What the brand has not built is the social infrastructure that converts individual loyalty into collective identity. Seventy percent of runners report feeling more motivated when running with others. Only fifteen percent participate in organized running communities regularly. The gap between those numbers is not a product problem. It is a platform problem.

03The Ask

Design a transformative platform that shifts the focus from gear to the emotional and social core of movement.

04The Problem

Running communities exist, but they operate at the wrong scale for most people. Large organized groups are intimidating. Generic fitness apps track performance without building connection. The social platforms that runners use were not designed for the specific dynamics of running, the shared route, the shared pace, the accountability that comes from knowing someone is meeting you at the corner at six in the morning. The infrastructure for that kind of connection does not exist at scale. Seventy percent of runners feel more motivated in groups. Fifteen percent have access to the kind of group that actually provides that motivation. The platform problem is not awareness. It is the absence of a system that makes the right connection at the right scale.

05Research Methods

User research examined the behavioral gap between runners who preferred group running in principle and those who participated in practice, identifying the specific friction points that prevented conversion. Community mapping analyzed the existing landscape of running clubs, fitness apps, and social platforms to locate where connection attempts were falling short and why. Pilot testing ran beta pods in Chicago and Berlin with real runners over a defined period, generating live data on pod retention, run completion rates, and the gamification mechanics that produced the strongest behavioral response. The pilot data was used to validate the pod-matching algorithm before full platform specification.

06Key Data / Findings

The motivation gap is the central finding: seventy percent of runners feel more motivated in a group, but only fifteen percent run in one regularly. The retention data from the pilot pods is the most commercially significant result. Pods of ten to twenty runners retained members at three times the rate of groups of thirty to fifty, confirming that scale is the enemy of connection, and that curation at the right size produces meaningfully different outcomes. Sixty-five percent of participants expressed interest in gamified challenges with tangible rewards. Product-based rewards drove forty percent higher purchase intent than cash prizes, establishing the direct commercial link between community participation and On's core business.

07The Insight

The barrier to running in a community is not fitness. Runners who run alone are not running alone because they prefer it. They are running alone because the alternative requires navigating a level of social friction, finding the right group, committing to a schedule, showing up to something that might not fit, that most people will not sustain. The insight is structural: remove the friction of finding the right people at the right time and the preference for community running expresses itself immediately. On's role is not to motivate people to run. It is to make it easier to run with others than to run alone.

08The Strategy

Apex operates through three interlocking pillars. Local Pods are curated groups of ten to twenty runners matched by goals, schedule, and geography, small enough for genuine accountability, consistent enough for real relationship formation. Competitive Gamification translates the social energy of pod membership into city-wide leaderboards and a tiered reward system that connects participation directly to On's product ecosystem. Technical Integration embeds Mapbox-powered navigation directly into active runs, giving pod members real-time awareness of each other's location and route progress during the run itself. The three pillars are designed to work together: the pod provides the relationship, the gamification provides the motivation to maintain it, and the navigation makes the run itself a shared experience.

09Concept / Design Work

The On Apex PWA is built around the Pod Dashboard as its central interface, a space where pod members see each other's profiles, upcoming runs, and collective progress toward shared goals, with behavioral tags like early bird and weekend warrior that reflect actual participation patterns rather than aspirational identity. AR navigation runs during active runs, displaying real-time route markers and pod member positions so runners maintain spatial awareness of the people they are running with. The reward system is tiered and product-anchored: On discount codes at the entry tier, free gear and partner benefits at higher participation levels. The full platform is specified in a molecular design system with complete token documentation and demonstrated through an interactive prototype covering pod formation, run execution, and reward redemption flows.

10Business Benefits

The pilot data projects a forty-five percent increase in local group run participation within six months of launch. The NPS from pilot participants was 82, a figure that reflects not satisfaction with a product but identification with a community. Participants who ran in Apex pods showed twenty-five percent higher demand for On gear than the broader consumer population, establishing a direct causal link between community participation and purchase behavior. The long-term commercial case is a retention argument: a runner who identifies as an Apex pod member is not a Nike customer waiting to be poached by a better shoe. She is an On community member whose loyalty is structured around something a competitor cannot replicate by releasing a better midsole.
