Executive Summary

Glenn Martens joined Diesel in 2020 and rebuilt the brand's creative authority in three years. The product is strong. The cultural moment is real. What the brand does not yet have is a physical statement that matches the scale of the renaissance. The Iceberg in Harajuku is that statement, a five-floor structure in the world's most design-literate retail market, designed to bring the ninety percent of Diesel's identity that has always existed below the surface into the light.

02Background

Diesel was founded in Molvena, Italy in 1978 by Renzo Rosso with a specific and unconventional proposition: denim as premium luxury rather than workwear. The brand built its first two decades on irreverence, craft, and a willingness to say things in advertising that other brands would not touch. That identity drifted in the 2010s under commercial pressure and an attempt to chase a more accessible market. The result was a brand that had lost the thread of what made it worth caring about. Martens' appointment as Creative Director restored the thread. His first collections were a recalibration, back to the archive, back to the denim craft, back to the specific kind of provocation that only makes sense coming from a brand with Diesel's history. The renaissance is not a rebrand. It is a return.

03The Ask

Capitalize on the current renaissance under Glenn Martens to cement Diesel's revitalized global status through a major physical statement in one of the world's most design-literate retail markets.

04The Problem

A standard flagship in Harajuku misses the point of the audience. Japanese luxury consumers are among the most discerning buyers in the world, not because they spend more, but because they read deeper. They do not purchase the object. They purchase the story behind the object, the craft evidence within it, and the cultural context that makes it worth possessing. A retail environment that presents product without presenting the depth of the brand that made the product is a missed conversation with exactly the audience most capable of having it. Diesel has ninety percent of itself that has never been made visible in a retail context. The Iceberg is the format that makes it present.

05Research Methods

Consumer behavior analysis examined the specific reasons Japanese shoppers continue to value physical retail at rates that outpace other major markets, identifying the experience qualities that a digital channel cannot replicate for this consumer. Competitive benchmarking analyzed recent flagship openings in Harajuku by Calvin Klein, Louis Vuitton, Gucci, and Jordan, mapping the spatial and experiential strategies each brand used and identifying where the category had left room. A cultural alignment audit assessed the specific resonances between Diesel's brand DNA, craft, irreverence, Italian warmth, archive depth, and the values that define Japanese luxury consumer behavior at the premium tier.

06Key Data / Findings

Japan's position as the world's fourth-largest economy provides the commercial foundation, but the more important finding is behavioral. Japanese consumers purchase luxury goods primarily as self-reward rather than status performance, which means the audience is buying for internal reasons, not external ones. That distinction is critical for brand strategy: a consumer buying to signal to others can be reached with visibility. A consumer buying to reward herself needs to be reached with depth. The Harajuku location validates the structural concept directly: the building is in the neighborhood where the most design-literate consumers in the market spend their time. The iceberg metaphor, ten percent visible, ninety percent submerged, is not a tagline. It is a precise description of how Diesel's brand has always operated, and how the right audience reads it.

07The Insight

What Diesel has always had, and what the Martens era has restored, is a specific kind of depth that most fashion brands do not possess. The archive exists. The craft philosophy exists. The cultural history of the brand as a challenger to the industry's own pretensions exists. None of it has ever been made spatially present in a retail context at the scale the brand warrants. Japanese consumers do not need that story explained to them. They need a building that makes it inhabitable. The insight is that the building is the product, and the product is the argument for everything Diesel has ever stood for.

08The Strategy

The Iceberg operates as a cultural hub rather than a retail store, organized around the principle that the brand experience is the primary offering and the product is the outcome of that experience. The spatial strategy balances Tokyo's industrial brutalism, the material language of the city's most respected retail architecture, with the warmth and tactility of Italian craft. Mandatory phone-free floors on selected levels enforce presence over documentation: the experience is not designed to be shared on social media while it is happening. It is designed to be inhabited. The brand earns its social presence through the quality of what visitors remember, not through the content they capture.

09Concept / Design Work

The building runs five floors. The ground floor is high-velocity retail with Fit Check–optimized lighting and a cash wrap carved from rough-hewn stone, setting the material register for everything above. Café 1978, with archive wall, library, and coworking space, sits in the mid-building volume where the brand's history becomes a social space. The Denim Lab is a glass-walled craft theater for laser distressing and vintage repair: work that is visible, participatory, and proof of Italian making. The Ghiaccio Ice Bar crowns the stack: a monolithic molded-glass bar that reads frozen, where phones return and word of mouth is earned after the visit. 10Business Benefits

A flagship in Harajuku at this level of ambition functions as a global press event that continues to generate coverage for the duration of its operation. The commercial case in Japan is secondary to the reputational case globally: a building this intentional, in this location, communicates that Diesel's renaissance is not a collection moment but a brand position. Within the Asian market, Japan's cultural influence operates as a distribution mechanism, the taste that forms in Tokyo travels to Seoul, Shanghai, and Singapore. A successful Iceberg flagship is not a single market investment. It is the first node of an Asian brand narrative with Harajuku as its origin point.
