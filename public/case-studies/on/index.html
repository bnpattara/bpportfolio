Executive Summary

On holds itself to a standard that most brands don't attempt. Every shoe is a precise answer to the question of what human movement should feel like — engineered in Switzerland, tested on trails and city blocks, finished to a level of detail that makes a runner choose On for the design before they ever read a spec sheet. That standard permeates the hardware. It has never touched the software.

Nike owns the aspiration layer with NRC. Strava owns the performance layer. On has no owned digital surface where its runners experience the brand at all. After the transaction, On disappears from its customer's life. The community that formed around On — the runners who see the shoe as identity, the club runners who coordinate on Instagram, the aesthetic athletes who post routes in CloudSurfers — lives entirely on other people's platforms, by other people's rules, in other people's visual language.

On Apex is the argument that the software layer deserves the same standard as the hardware. A community-first PWA built to On's precision — Swiss-minimalist, instrument-grade, no gamification chrome, no follower counts as status. A platform where movement is the primary act, the shoe is the identity unit, and the community is built on shared miles rather than shared metrics. Not a Strava competitor. Not a data play. A software product finally worthy of the shoes it tracks.

This case study demonstrates zero-to-one product strategy for a brand that does not yet have a community platform, multi-persona user modeling across three distinct runner psychographics, Phase-gated feature prioritization with explicit P0/P1/P2 reasoning, and the discipline of separating commerce from community — every purchase trigger in the app is contextual, never promotional.

01The Problem

The hardware is flawless. The software layer doesn't exist.

On's brand is built on a specific belief: that human movement is the highest form of self-expression, and that the objects which enable that movement deserve to be built accordingly. The CloudTec sole geometry, the Helion foam, the Speedboard — these are not features. They are a philosophy made physical. Runners choose On not because it ranks highest in a comparison chart but because wearing it feels like a considered decision about how they move through the world. That level of intention lives in every product On ships. It has never existed anywhere else in the brand experience.

After a runner buys On shoes, the brand's presence in their life effectively ends. They train in Strava. They share on Instagram. They organize club runs in WhatsApp groups. They post to Reddit communities that On has no relationship with. The running that happens in On shoes — the data, the community, the identity, the culture — all of it accumulates somewhere else. Nike understood this fifteen years ago and built NRC. Adidas understood it and built Runtastic. On has remained, at the digital layer, a transaction. A beautiful transaction. But only a transaction.

The competitive context makes the gap more urgent. Nike Run Club is not a competitor On can beat on fitness features — it has a decade of head start and Nike's full media apparatus behind it. On cannot win by building a better GPS tracker or a more gamified leaderboard. What On can build — what only On can build — is a platform that holds itself to On's own standard. Minimal. Precise. Designed around movement as a human act, not a performance metric. The question On Apex answers is not "how do we compete with Strava?" It is: "what does software look like when it's built the way On builds shoes?"

02The Insight

Movement is the act. The shoe is the object. The platform is the witness.

“On runners don’t run to compete with each other. They run to become a version of themselves. The platform needs to understand the difference.”

The insight that separates On Apex from every existing running platform is a belief about why people run. Strava is built on the premise that runners are primarily motivated by performance comparison — KOMs, segments, leaderboards, the implicit question of how you rank. Nike Run Club is built on aspiration — the brand halo, the motivation overlays, the celebrity run guides. Both are valid products for large portions of the running market. Neither is the right product for On's runner, who chose On precisely because they are not that runner. The On runner chose a shoe designed in Switzerland, engineered for the sensation of movement, finished without ornamentation. That choice is a statement about values. The platform needs to honor it.

The second insight is about what makes On's position defensible. Any app can add GPS tracking. Any app can build a social feed. What no other app can do is make the shoe the identity unit — because no other app has On's shoe portfolio, On's brand permission, and the specific psychographic of runner that On attracts. The Cloudmonster runner and the Cloudgo runner are different people with different relationships to performance, aesthetics, and community. Apex knows the difference at the data level. That specificity is the moat. Not the technology. The specificity.

On runners run to express something — an aesthetic, a discipline, a relationship to their own body in motion. The platform's job is to witness that, not to rank it. No KOM. No trophy. No gamification chrome.
Insight 02 · The Craft Standard

On holds its hardware to a standard most brands don't attempt. The software deserves the same. Every screen in Apex is built to a single question: does this earn its presence? If not, it doesn't ship.
Insight 03 · The Untaken Territory
Open
Nike owns aspiration. Strava owns data. The territory no platform owns — aesthetic intentionality, movement as identity, design-forward running culture — is exactly where On lives. Apex plants the flag there.

The design system reflects this thesis at every level. The split mode — white for community and identity, black for the active run — is not a dark mode feature. It is a statement that the run is a different register of experience from everything that surrounds it. The scanning line on the run screen, the instrument-grade metric grid, the blinking colon that switches rather than fades — these are not aesthetic choices. They are the software equivalent of the Speedboard's flex geometry: every element doing exactly what it needs to do, nothing performing for its own sake. The app is built the way On builds shoes. That is the insight and the product simultaneously.

03The Users

Three runners. One relationship to movement.

What unites On's three distinct runner psychographics is not their pace, their mileage, or their competitive drive. It is a shared belief that the act of running means something — that it is a form of self-expression that deserves to be treated with care. The brand they chose reflects that belief. The platform they use every day doesn't. On Apex is built for the runner who made that choice deliberately, and who notices when the software around the hardware doesn't match.

PM Lens · The Persona Strategy Is a Brand Argument

All three personas share one thing: they chose On with intention. They are not runners who happened to buy On shoes — they are runners whose values are legible in the shoes they bought. The product strategy follows from this. A platform built for Elena cannot prioritize KOMs. A platform built for Marcus cannot be decorative. A platform built for Jamie cannot fragment the community across tabs and features. The two-mode design system — white for identity and community, black for the act of running — is the architectural expression of this understanding. The modes don't just change colors. They change what the screen believes the runner is doing, and behave accordingly.

04The Product

Six P0 features. Four P1. One rule.

The PRD scopes three phases with an explicit dependency chain. Phase 1 ships the core loop — Activity Log, Shoe Garage, Social Feed, Onboarding, Pre-Run Screen, Profile. Each feature enables the next: the run produces data; the data feeds the Garage; the Garage earns the commerce trigger; the community retains the runner who generates the next run. Phase 2 adds the social infrastructure — Apex Clubs, Challenges, Events, Routes — but only once Phase 1 has produced the behavioral data that justifies their design. Phase 3 integrates commerce. The rule: no Phase 2 feature exists before Phase 1 is proven. No Phase 3 feature enters the app without the contextual data to justify its placement. Shipping in the wrong order produces a product where features exist but don't connect.

05Design System

A Molecular Design System built for two registers.

The Molecular Design System was built alongside the PRD, not after it. Every token, component, and pattern was derived from a specific user need — the split mode system exists because of the persona strategy, not because dark mode is fashionable. Light mode (#F7F7F3 base, #FFFFFF card surface) serves the architectural register: community, discovery, identity. Dark mode (#050505 base, #0E0E0E card surface) serves the instrument register: active run, pre-run GPS lock, onboarding threshold moment.

Three typefaces carry three distinct roles. Bebas Neue is display-only — large numbers, screen titles, the 116px weekly km that is the home screen's entire argument. DM Mono handles all metadata: pace labels, distances, dates, status indicators. DM Sans carries body copy, names, and conversational text. No typeface crosses into another's territory. The volt accent (#E8FF3C) appears a maximum of three times per screen — always on a dark surface, always earning its presence.

PM Lens · The Craft Standard Applied to Software

On's hardware is built around a single question: does this earn its presence? The Speedboard flex geometry earns its presence by changing how energy transfers through the foot strike. The CloudTec pods earn their presence by decoupling landing and takeoff in a way no other sole geometry achieves. Nothing decorates. Everything performs. The design system for Apex applies the same discipline to every token and component. Zero border-radius is not a style preference — it is the refusal of the rounded corner's implicit message that this is approachable consumer software. The volt scarcity rule is not restraint for its own sake — it is the understanding that a signal loses meaning when it appears everywhere. The blinking colon on the run screen uses step-end timing, not ease-in, because instruments switch; they do not transition. Every one of these decisions is the software asking the same question the hardware asks: does this earn its presence? If not, it doesn't ship.

06 Key Screens
Five screens.
One coherent product argument.

Each screen is a direct PM decision mapped to a PRD feature. None is a UI exercise. Each one closes a specific gap in On's data infrastructure — from the moment a runner opens the app to the moment they retire a shoe.

What this screen does

Home is an architectural screen — one number owns it. The 116px weekly km (rendered here at 72px for the SVG scale) is the first thing the eye resolves before anything else loads. White cards float on the warm #F7F7F3 base with real drop-shadow elevation — three distinct depth layers visible on a single screen. Volt appears exactly three times: today's week bar, the runs count block, the shoe nudge icon. The volt scarcity rule is the design system enforcing the brand argument at the component level.

01 / 05
What this screen does

The Active Run screen is an instrument-grade cockpit. The entire white-mode app inverts to near-black the moment you press Start — the transition is the product. A scanning line animation gives the screen live-data signal without a spinner. The blinking colon is step-end timing, not ease-in — it switches, because instruments switch. Zone bars update in real time as the run simulation progresses. Volt appears once: the currently active zone bar.

02 / 05
What this screen does

The post-run summary is an editorial screen — designed to be screenshotted. The black header with ghost type creates depth without texture. The PR badge volt-pop animation arrives 750ms after mount — delayed, so it lands as a reward. The shoe appears at the bottom of every summary, with a mileage bar and km remaining. This is the commerce loop: no CTA, no ad, just a quiet record of wear that earns the replace prompt when the time comes.

03 / 05
What this screen does

The Shoe Garage is the feature no other running app has — because no other running app has On's shoe portfolio. Every run is attributed to a specific model. Mileage bars shift from black to amber to red as thresholds approach. The retire prompt uses amber, not red: retirement is completion, not failure. The only commerce surface in the entire app lives here — a Shop on.com link earned by 487 km of behavioral data. The Cyclon button closes the sustainability loop On has built at the product level.

04 / 05
What this screen does

The Community Hub is the most complex screen in the product — a tabbed surface containing Clubs (geo-located, with leaderboard and RSVP), Challenges (distance/elevation/streak, with On-exclusive drops), Events (On-sponsored races and pop-up runs), and Routes (community-submitted and curated, with shoe recommendations). The reaction system — Push, Solid, Pace, Zone, Respect — replaces hearts. The PRD constraint is explicit: no follower counts as status, no KOM, no trophy system. Community is built on shared miles, not competitive metrics.

Active Run — instrument-grade cockpit
Active Run Black Mode Live Simulation

The run screen is black. A scanning line at 3.4s interval gives the screen live-data signal. Pace fluctuates ±8 sec/km per tick. HR drifts ±4 bpm. Cadence ±3 spm. The Finish button is volt-on-black — the only bright thing on the screen.

Post-Run — the shareable moment
Post-Run White Mode Returns Editorial Commerce Hook

The world returns to white. The post-run summary is an editorial screen — designed to be screenshotted and shared. PR badge volt-pop animation arrives 750ms after mount. The share sheet generates a branded card with ghost type and a volt On Apex tag. Toast fires on save.

Shoe Registration — Flow 2, fully built
Shoe Reg Order Sync 4 Steps Toast on Complete

Club RSVP — community in action
Clubs Leaderboard RSVP Sheet Toast on Join

Community Hub → Clubs tab. Tap a club card to open the detail sheet. Leaderboard shows ranked members with your position highlighted. Next club run displayed with location and headcount. RSVP button fires a medium haptic and a toast: "You're in! See you Saturday ✓". The community loop: run → log → rank → coordinate → run again. No follower counts. No KOM. No gamification chrome.

Retire + Replace — the commerce loop
Garage Amber Signal Deep Link Only Cyclon Option

When a shoe crosses 75% of its mileage goal, the bar turns amber. At 90%, red. The retire prompt appears in the Garage as an inverted black card with an amber dot — completion, not alarm. Two options: "Shop on.com →" (deep link, no in-app checkout, ever) and "Cyclon ♻" (circular return program). The Cyclon sheet shows the subscription details and a join CTA. Toast fires on join: "Cyclon request sent ♻". This is the entire commerce strategy — contextual, earned, never promotional.

07Decision Log

What was built, what was deferred, and why.

Product decisions are only credible when documented with the tradeoff accepted — not just the choice made. This log covers every significant decision in the PRD, design system, and prototype, including the features that were cut from Phase 1 and the reasons they were not premature inclusions.

PWA — Phase 1 Architecture Phase 1 ships as a Progressive Web App: service worker caching for offline run logging, Web Geolocation API for GPS tracking, Web Vibration API for haptics, manifest.json for home screen install, aggressive install prompting from within on.com. No App Store submission. No native binary. No third-party distribution dependency. The case for PWA in Phase 1 is a distribution argument, not a capability argument.

On's acquisition channel is on.com, not the App Store. The runner who chooses On does so deliberately — they arrive via the website, they research CloudTec, they know the model they want before they buy it. The install prompt inside that owned relationship converts an already-engaged audience. An App Store listing converts a cold search audience surrounded by Nike ads, Strava featured placements, and Garmin promotions. The two distribution surfaces are not equivalent. On's is better.

App Store review cycle kills Phase 1 iteration velocity. A new product finding product-market fit needs to ship fixes and experiments weekly. App Store review averages 1–3 days per submission, with rejection risk on each. A PWA deploys in minutes, updates invisibly to the user, and iterates without a gatekeeper. For Phase 1 — where the onboarding flow, reaction system, and shoe registration UX will all need rapid iteration based on real user behavior — this is not a minor advantage.

Note on App Store commerce fees: Apple's 30% commission applies exclusively to digital goods and in-app subscriptions — not physical product transactions. Fashion apps (ASOS, Farfetch, Depop) and running apps (Nike) sell physical goods natively without App Store commission. This is not a factor in the PWA decision for Apex, whose only commerce surface is a deep-link to on.com for physical shoe purchases.

Capability tradeoffs accepted: Web Geolocation API cannot run in the background — the screen must stay active during a run. Push notifications are less reliable than native APNs. No HealthKit or Garmin Connect API access without a native layer. These are real costs. They are acceptable in Phase 1 because the core loop — log a run, track a shoe, build community — does not require background GPS or wearable sync. Phase 3 ships native. 		Shipped · Phase 1
Native App — Phase 3 Roadmap React Native or Swift/Kotlin native app with HealthKit and Garmin Connect integration, background GPS tracking, reliable APNs push notifications, Apple Watch complication for live run metrics, and App Store presence for brand legitimacy signal. Deferred to Phase 3. Native is the right long-term architecture. The question is sequencing.

Phase 1 needs to prove the core loop works — that runners register shoes, log runs, and return to the community surface — before investing in the higher engineering cost of a dual-platform native build. The behavioral data from Phase 1 (WAU, shoe registration rate, session frequency) directly informs the native app's feature prioritization. Building native before that data exists produces a well-engineered product optimized for the wrong things.

The Phase 3 trigger is retention: when 35% WAU holds for two consecutive quarters and shoe registration exceeds 70%, the product has proven retention sufficient to justify native investment. At that point, the App Store presence shifts from a distribution channel to a brand legitimacy signal — a top-ranked running app with On's name on it is itself a brand asset, not just a download mechanism.

Precedent: Strava launched mobile web before native. Whoop shipped a simplified app before its current native architecture. The pattern for fitness products is validate on web, scale on native. 		Deferred · Phase 3
Scroll Snap Carousels Shoe Garage and Route Library use CSS scroll-snap-type: mandatory horizontal carousels with dot position indicators, replacing the prior vertical stacks. Each card peeks the next at the right edge. Horizontal carousels hide content below the fold. The peek signals scrollability. Shoe cards at full card-width encourage treating each shoe as a primary object, not a list item — which reinforces the shoe-as-identity-layer thesis at the component level. Shipped
Commerce Constraint Deep-link to on.com only. No in-app checkout, no product catalog, no advertising in any phase. The only commerce surface is the Garage retire prompt, triggered by mileage data. Limits near-term revenue potential. Required: On's commerce channel is on.com. Building a competing in-app channel would create a business conflict with On's existing retail model. The commerce constraint protects the partnership and keeps the app's incentive structure aligned — the platform earns on engagement, not transaction. Shipped
Haptic API Web Vibration API wired to every TapBtn with type parameters: light (8ms) for standard taps, medium (18ms) for confirmations and successes, warning (12+8+12ms) for destructive actions. Vibration API not supported across all browsers and PWA contexts. Implemented with graceful fallback — haptic() returns silently if navigator.vibrate is unavailable. The interaction quality improvement on supported devices justifies the implementation cost. Shipped
Challenges Drops Challenge UI with On-exclusive drop rewards is built and visible in the prototype. The actual drop fulfillment infrastructure (inventory integration, winner verification, distribution) is deferred to Phase 2 post-launch. The drops mechanic requires inventory coordination with On's merchandising team. The UI is built to demonstrate the product argument. The operational infrastructure is a Phase 2 dependency on challenge volume data from Phase 1. Deferred · Ops
On Account SSO SSO indicator is present in Profile ("Connected to On account · Orders sync automatically"). The actual OAuth integration with On's account infrastructure is not implemented in the prototype. Requires API access to On's production systems — out of scope for a conceptual prototype. The UI, the flow (Order Sync step in Shoe Registration), and the data model are all specified. Implementation is a Phase 1 engineering dependency, not a product decision. Deferred · Eng
PWA Manifest + Service Worker PWA install banner implemented in the prototype (fires 3 seconds after onboarding completion). Full manifest.json, service worker offline caching for run sessions, and background sync for post-run data not yet implemented. 45% PWA install rate is a PRD success metric requiring the complete manifest implementation. The banner in the prototype demonstrates the prompt strategy and conversion moment. Full service worker implementation — offline run logging, background sync queue, push notification registration — is a Phase 1 engineering deliverable. The prototype proves the UX. Engineering builds the infrastructure. Deferred · Eng

PM Lens · What This Project Demonstrates

On Apex demonstrates the discipline of zero-to-one product strategy for a brand that has never shipped a community platform. The PRD makes 11 hard decisions with documented tradeoffs, specifies 6 success metrics with measurement methodology, and draws explicit phase boundaries between MVP and roadmap. The design system translates those decisions into a visual language — the split mode system, the volt scarcity rule, the zero border-radius constraint — where every token represents a product decision rendered in CSS. The prototype builds 14 screens across three user flows without a single feature added for aesthetic reasons. Every interaction pattern is specified in the MDS, every screen has a single declared primary action, and every commerce surface is contextually earned rather than promotionally placed.

What this project argues about On as a product opportunity: the runners who choose On have already made a statement about how they want to experience movement. They chose a brand that holds its hardware to an unusual standard — not the loudest, not the most marketed, but the most considered. That same runner has never had a software experience that reflects the choice they made. Every day they open Strava, or Instagram, or a WhatsApp group to organize a club run, is a day the brand's standard stops at the box. On Apex is the argument that it shouldn't. The data is a consequence. The community is a consequence. The commerce is a consequence. The thing itself is a software product built the way On builds shoes: with nothing unnecessary, nothing decorative, and nothing that doesn't earn its presence.

What this project argues about PM practice: the strongest product decisions are the ones that can be traced back to a belief. The volt scarcity rule traces back to the belief that a signal earns its authority through scarcity. The Pre-Run screen traces back to the belief that the transition into the run deserves ceremony — it is the moment the platform signals that it understands what running means to this user. The zero border-radius traces back to On's own design language, which is not approachable consumer aesthetics but Swiss instrument precision. These are not design choices made in a vacuum. They are a belief system made tangible in product decisions, made visible in a design system, and made interactive in a prototype. That is the discipline this case study demonstrates — and the standard On Apex was built to meet.
