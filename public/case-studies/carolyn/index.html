00 · Executive Summary

Calvin Klein has thirty years of cultural capital and no named hero product. Every major competitor — Hermès, Chanel, Dior, Fendi — has at least one object that outlives its campaigns, anchors its brand equity, and compounds in value on the secondary market. Calvin Klein has none. That gap is not an accessories problem. It is an equity architecture problem.

The brief in one sentence: “Make the bag she was already carrying — and name it after her.” Each of the six decisions maps to a distinct PM discipline: structural reframe, user grounding, evaluation framework, product specification, go-to-market sequencing, and sensitivity risk management. The output is a launch-ready recommendation with documented tradeoffs at every fork. Two things make this project unusually strong. First: the muse is not borrowed. Carolyn Bessette worked inside Calvin Klein before she was famous — which means the association is constitutive, not contractual. Second: the cultural moment is already moving. A new generation is finding her through archival photography and '90s New York reappraisal. The brand is not creating the conversation. This is about joining it with authority.

01 The User — Who buys this bag. Not a demographic — a person.

The primary buyer for The Carolyn at launch

Product decisions without a user are positioning exercises. Before the spec, before the silhouette, before the price point — there has to be a person. Here is the primary buyer for The Carolyn at launch.

THE BUYER Maya, 34. Senior associate at a midsize architecture firm in Manhattan's Flatiron district. Annual income $115K.

Maya has owned the same four bags for three years. A black Polène tote she uses daily. A vintage Coach crossbody she found at a market in Paris. A Madewell zip pouch. And a structured shoulder bag she bought at a department store that she's been meaning to replace since the hardware started tarnishing.

She's aware of luxury. She spent two months considering a Wandler before deciding it felt derivative. She follows the COS and Toteme Instagram accounts but doesn't shop them often. She thinks about the Prada Re-Edition but the logo placement feels too legible. She wants something she doesn't have to explain.

She came across Carolyn Bessette-Kennedy through a Substack post about '90s minimalism and spent an hour looking at archival photographs. She saved three of them. She described them to a friend as 'the clearest argument I've ever seen for buying less.'

She is not a luxury customer. She is an aspiring quality customer. The difference matters for pricing, for distribution, and for how the bag is introduced — because she will not respond to fanfare, and she will detect a marketing tone immediately.

DimensionProfileImplication for The Carolyn
Income$100–130K individual$450–650 is a considered purchase, not an impulse. She researches. She waits.
Consideration setWandler, Métier, Destree, Polène, vintage luxuryShe's choosing against quiet, European, design-forward brands. The Carolyn needs to feel equally intentional.
Decision driverLongevity, material quality, no legible brandingInterior stamp only is the right call. She doesn't want to carry a logo — she wants to carry a decision.

A secondary buyer exists: the established luxury customer trading down from Chanel or Celine who finds the $550 price point surprisingly honest. She validates the positioning. If she picks it up, the editorial story writes itself.

02 Problem Diagnosis — Is this an accessory problem or an equity architecture problem?

This isn’t an accessories gap. It’s an equity architecture gap.

DECISION TYPE: STRUCTURAL REFRAME

Most brands would brief an accessories designer. The first move here was different — and that difference changes every decision downstream.

Calvin Klein’s cultural capital lives in campaigns. Three decades of iconic advertising built extraordinary recall — but recall tied to campaigns spikes and retreats. It doesn’t compound. What compounds is an object: a named product that outlives any single season and anchors brand equity over decades.

Every luxury house with long-term commercial resilience has a named product that outlives its campaigns. The Birkin. The Kelly. The Lady Dior. The 2.55. These aren't accessories — they're institutional arguments. They say: we have been here long enough to name something after a woman, and that woman was right about everything. Calvin Klein has no equivalent.

"That's not an accessories gap. It's an equity architecture gap — and the two require completely different briefs."

Decision 01 — Problem Framing
Is Calvin Klein missing a bag, or missing a brand icon?
A Brief the accessories team on a new seasonal bag. Solves the product gap without touching brand architecture. Low risk, low reward, no compounding equity. The bag gets seasonal attention and seasonal shelf life.
B — SELECTED Frame it as an equity architecture problem. Build a named hero bag designed to carry the brand for 10+ years — independent of any single campaign or season. Harder to execute, requires a sensitivity framework, and demands long-term price and distribution discipline. The only option that builds something durable.

Selected: B. The Meaning Transfer Model describes how cultural meaning moves from the world to a person, then from a person to a product, then from a product to a consumer. A campaign transfers meaning for a season. A named product transfers it permanently. Option A produces a bag. Option B produces a mechanism.

03 Muse Selection — The insight came first. The framework stress-tested it.
DECISION TYPE: INSIGHT-FIRST EVALUATION

The evaluation wasn't about fame. It was about which association could anchor a permanent product line without dating it, diluting it, or requiring the brand to respond to external events.

Process note: the insight that Carolyn Bessette was the right answer came before the framework. She worked inside Calvin Klein before she was famous. Her wardrobe is the brand's design language made personal. The connection is constitutive, not borrowed — and no other brand can make that claim. The framework below was built to stress-test that insight, not to generate it.

Decision 02 — Muse Archetype
Which type of woman can anchor this product for 10+ years?
ArchetypeExclusivityLongevityOwnabilityVerdict
Campaign Girl (historic CK face)Low — shared equity across multiple brandsMedium — archive is strong but reads as endorsement, not authorshipLow — other brands hold equivalent claimsCUT
Now Star (current musician, athlete, actor)Medium — real-time relevanceLow — reputation volatility, trend dependency. Viral, then dated.Low — celebrity-as-product theme, not authorshipCUT

The Source Credibility Model holds that celebrity-product association succeeds when it scores high on both expertise and trustworthiness in the product domain. Carolyn Bessette wasn't adjacent to fashion — she operated inside it professionally, at this specific brand. The Match-Up Hypothesis adds a third requirement: the association must feel inevitable, not constructed. For The Carolyn, it's the woman who was already wearing the brand before anyone was watching.

"The question is never 'who is famous?' It's 'who is right?' Those are different searches and they produce different products."

The timing argument is structural, not opportunistic. A new generation is discovering Carolyn Bessette-Kennedy through archival photography — not as nostalgia, but as aspiration. That conversation is happening without Calvin Klein. This is about arriving with authority before the moment closes.

04 Product Specification — 'Minimal' is a brand word. This is what it means when it has to ship.
DECISION TYPE: PRD AUTHORSHIP

The design brief is one sentence: make the bag she was already carrying. Everything else is the work of translating a brand aesthetic into six concrete, non-negotiable parameters — and defining the exclusions with equal rigor.

Decision 03 — Who Writes the Spec
Does strategy defer to design, or does strategy write the PRD?
A Defer specificity to the design team. Brand provides muse reference and aesthetic direction; product team translates. Risk: without hard constraints, 'minimal' becomes a style direction rather than a product argument. The bag risks looking like any other clean shoulder bag with a famous name applied.
B — SELECTED Strategy writes the PRD. Define all six parameters before the design team touches a sketch. Define what this product is NOT as rigorously as what it is. The exclusions are the discipline — what separates a brief from a mood board.

Selected: B. The spec below is the brief. Everything the design team produces gets measured against it.

ParameterSpecification
FormStructured shoulder bag. Single adjustable flat strap. Medium carry. Not a status bag, not a micro bag. A working bag with architectural presence — fits phone, wallet, keys, paperback.
MaterialFull-grain smooth leather. Launch in bone/ivory only. No exotic skins, no obvious texture plays at launch. Pebbled and alternative materials enter in year two once the silhouette has equity independent of colorway.
HardwareBrushed gold, one point. Architectural rectangular clasp. No logo hardware. Quality communicated through geometry, not branding. A single hardware decision signals intention.
BrandingInterior stamp only. CALVIN KLEIN / THE CAROLYN, blind-embossed. No exterior logo. The bag is recognizable by proportions and character.
SilhouetteTrapezoid body, envelope flap. Clean flap with no seam decoration. Structural interest from the trapezoidal lean — tailored precision without decorative elements. Full silhouette evaluation in Section 05.
Price$450–$650 retail. No markdowns year one. Aspirational without being exclusionary. A New York bag, not an Upper East Side bag. Maya can stretch to $550. She cannot justify $850. The price is a positioning statement, not a margin exercise.

Clean envelope edge — no seam decoration. Form carries the argument. Tone-on-tone magnetic closure. Hardware restraint as product philosophy.

The exclusions below are not self-evidently correct. Each one is a commercial decision with a real cost. Naming the cost is part of the discipline.

The Resale Argument

The silhouette decision has a financial dimension beyond aesthetics. Structured flap bags in neutral leathers consistently outperform totes and hobos on resale platforms. The Birkin did not hit 134% retention in year three. It took four decades of deliberate supply restriction and brand stewardship. The Carolyn's resale targets are derived from closer analogues — design-forward bags from non-heritage houses that established secondary market presence within 36 months through editorial seeding and supply discipline.

Hermès Birkin134%40 years of deliberate supply restriction + institutional brand infrastructure
Hermès Kelly115%Decades of consistent positioning; singular silhouette; no design variation

06 Launch Sequencing — One colorway or two. The sequencing logic and the metrics.
DECISION TYPE: GO-TO-MARKET

The sage-blue-grey had cultural justification — it appears in the archival wardrobe and the reference is genuine. The argument for launching it alongside bone was real. The argument against was a sequencing logic, not a taste call.

Decision 05 — Launch Colorway Strategy
Do we launch bone only, or bone + sage?
A Launch bone + sage simultaneously. Both colorways have brief justification. Broader assortment increases first-season conversion. Risk: splits the identity story before it's established. Two colorways at launch reads as retail logic, not brand logic — optimizing for conversion instead of iconography.
B — SELECTED Launch bone only. Build the identity around a singular colorway first. Introduce sage in Resort year two framed as the permanent line expanding — not the brand hedging. Options at launch split the story.

Selected: B. Sage is a good color. It's not a launch color. The distinction is between what's correct and what's correct right now.

07 Sensitivity Framework — How to name a product after a deceased person. The single test — applied.
DECISION TYPE: RISK MANAGEMENT

Nobody asked for a sensitivity framework. Building one anyway is the PM move — because the reputational and legal risks here are real, and a legally clean launch can still read as exploitation if the tone is wrong.

Decision 06 — Sensitivity Handling
What's the single test every piece of creative must pass?
A Standard legal review only. Clear the name use, confirm no likeness rights issues, proceed. Risk: legal clearance is not reputational clearance.
B — SELECTED Build a sensitivity framework with a single test. Does this honor her contribution to this brand's aesthetic, or does it exploit her story? If there is any ambiguity, revise. Apply to copy, imagery, retail language, press materials, and campaign concept before production begins.

Selected: B. The legal and the ethical are separate problems. Both need frameworks. The single-question test aligns a large creative team without a 30-page style guide — and it forces a genuine answer rather than a compliance check.

Legal note: use of a first name alone in a product context does not constitute likeness use in most jurisdictions and does not require estate clearance. Any use of imagery or specific biographical details in marketing materials requires formal clearance. Strategic recommendation: avoid likeness use entirely. The product's story lives in its aesthetic and historical connection to the brand — not in biographical narrative.

Do

  • Honor her contribution to the brand's aesthetic — she was part of this before anyone was watching.
  • Keep all copy factual and spare. Short sentences. Present tense. No sentiment.
  • Let the product carry the tribute. The bag is the statement.
  • Maintain editorial control over all placements at launch.
  • Initiate private outreach to the Kennedy family before any public announcement.
  • Treat this as a permanent line addition from day one in positioning, pricing, and distribution.

Do not

  • Reference personal details of her private life in any marketing context.
  • Use imagery from her personal archive without formal estate clearance.
  • Market this as commemorative, memorial, or tribute — ever.
  • Create limited editions, tribute variations, or anniversary SKUs.
  • Time launch to coincide with biographical media, documentaries, or death anniversaries.
  • Respond reactively to breaking cultural moments about her in any brand channel.
The Framework Applied — Three Creative Scenarios

A framework that isn't tested against real creative decisions is a checklist. Here are three scenarios and how the single question resolves each one.

SCENARIO A — FAILS
Proposed copy: 'In memory of a woman who changed fashion forever.' Does this honor her contribution or exploit her story? Fails immediately. 'In memory' frames the product as tribute. 'Changed fashion forever' is biographical superlative. The copy is about her life, not about the bag. Revise entirely.
SCENARIO B — PASSES
Proposed copy: 'She knew before anyone else. New York. 1994. Still.' Passes. Factual (she was in New York in 1994 wearing this aesthetic before it had a name), present tense ('Still'), lets the audience make the connection independently. Doesn't name her. Doesn't eulogize. Implies.
SCENARIO C — AMBIGUOUS → CUT
Proposed imagery: archival photograph of CBK on the street in Manhattan. The photograph is accurate to the aesthetic argument. But using it requires estate clearance, and the act of using an archival personal photograph positions the brand as a biographer. Resolution: do not use archival imagery. The product photograph carries the argument. The product is the evidence.

Copy direction that passes the test consistently: short, declarative, present tense, no biography, no sentiment. Let the audience arrive at the reference independently. The copy should reward recognition without demanding it.

08 PM Lens — What this project is actually demonstrating.

A complete product strategy decision stack — not a design concept

The Carolyn is a complete product strategy decision stack — not a design concept. The deliverable is not a bag. It's a decision-making framework that moves from structural diagnosis through launch-ready specification, with documented tradeoffs at every fork and a risk management layer that most brand briefs don't include.

This case study proves the work can move across the full spectrum: from cultural insight and structural reframe through user grounding, PRD authorship, design evaluation, go-to-market sequencing, and sensitivity risk management. Each section pre-answers a different PM interview question. Together they prove a range that a single digitally-native project cannot.

"The project isn't 'I designed a bag for Calvin Klein.' It's 'I found the structural gap in the brand's equity architecture, defined the brief, and ran every decision required to ship the recommendation — with the reasoning visible at every step.'"

The Carolyn sits in a different category from the other case studies. Nike SNKRS, Vault by Saks, On Apex — those are digitally-native problems. This is a physical product with a brand strategy layer, a user model, a sensitivity framework, and a long-term resale argument. It shows that the same PM thinking applies regardless of the medium — and that the most important decisions are often the ones about what to name things and who they're for.

The work isn't "I designed a bag for Calvin Klein." It's "I found the structural gap in the brand's equity architecture, defined the brief, and ran every decision required to ship a recommendation — with the reasoning visible at every step."
